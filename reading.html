Recommended reading

Markuss G. Ķēniņš

Mathematical

Mathematics is probably the toughest queen one can choose to tame; mathematical reasoning is at its core highly abstract and the goal is to develop a highly sophisticated ability to reason purely abstractly and rigorously. I believe the following serve will aid well in achieving this purpose.

  1. Preliminaries.

    While still in school, I suggest focusing on developing routine mathematical skills; you should be able to do elementary algebra very quickly and it will serve you well in the future. The best way to practice formal reasoning is via the geometry of Euclid; a very good course on axiomatic geometry is vital to train the mind (even if Euclidean geometry as such is hardly used thereafter). The Soviet school produced some of the best school literature on geometry; one resource is V. Gusev et al., Solving problems in geometry, archive link; another, from the American Olympiad school this time: H.S.M. Coxeter, S.L. Greitzer, Geometry revisited, pdf link. You should also learn elementary calculus; a rigorous treatment is given in M. Spivak, Calculus, 3rd ed., archive link; but any calculus book will suffice – I suggest Soviet ones (or Latvian written before 2000), e.g. A.F. Bermant, I.G. Aramanovich, Mathematical analysis, archive link (by no means the best resource). In my mind, elementary mathematics ends with calculus.

  2. First taste.

    Mathematics is built on sets; naïve set theory is thus the first resource of pure mathematics; I recommend the brilliant lecture notes on set theory, arithmetic, and elementary modular arithmetic by R. van der Veen and J. Top, Sets and numbers, pdf link. You now possess the tools to study the first real subject of pure mathematics – real analysis; a tame text that guides you through the theory and opens the gates to other maths texts is S. Abbott, Understanding analysis, archive link. This may then be easily followed by generalising the theorems about real numbers to metric spaces – sets with a distance function (metric) for any two points; an elementary resource is Sutherland, Introduction to etric and topological spaces, 2nd ed., archive link (1st ed.). Alternatively, you can choose to forego the separated treatment and jump straight into the metric theory (or read it immediately after Abbott / a few chapters of Sutherland) by reading the master himself, W. Rudin, Principles of mathematical analysis, archive link; I find it to be a great resource for highly condensed proofs and cleaning up the theory in one's mind. Real analysis for physicists is captured in the volumes of S.M. Nikolsky, A course of mathematical anylsis, vol. 1, archive link, vol. 2, archive link.

  3. First algebra.

    One of the gigantic disciplines of mathematics is abstract algebra; in my mind its study begins with elementary linear algebra – study of maps that send sums to sums and scalar multiples to scalar multiples of just the vector. If you look for a standard book, you will find the theory to consist of computing matrix products, eigenvalues, and other nasty and idiotically robotic computations; this is NOT linear algebra – it is a failed attempt of matrix theory. Linear algebra should be about the maps and not matrices – a matrix is just a special representation of a map. A fantastic resource is the lecture notes by M. Stoll, Linear algebra I, pdf link, followed up by Linear algebra II, pdf link. (Matrices are introduced in § 11.)

  4. Dynamics.

    L. Elsgolts, Differential equations and the calculus of variations, archive link. А.Ф. Филиппов, Сборник задач по дифференциальным уравнениям. Ижевск-Регулярная и хаотическая динамика. I.M. Gel'fand, S.V. Fomin, Calculus of variations, archive link. Some dynamical systems resource; most seem to be elementary. For engineers, linear systems and control references may be of interest.

  5. Algebra proper.

    An aboslutely brilliant modern treatment, introducing elementary category theory: P. Aluffi, Algebra: chapter 0. The original treatise on teaching abstract algebra (1931): B.I. van der Waerden, Moderne Algebra, archive link (there are translations in English from the 2nd edition onwards). Some resource on category theory. It seems that much depth lies under number theory and algebratic topology.

  6. Analysis proper.

    Measure theory: W. Rudin, Real and complex analysis; V.I. Bogachev, Measure theory. Functional analysis: W. Rudin, Functional analysis; V.I. Bogachev, O.G. Smolyanov, Real and functional analysis, published as vol. 4 in the Springer Moscow Lectures series. Multivariable: lecture notes of R. van der Veen, Multivariable analysis. Manifolds & differential geometry: M. Spivak, A comprehensive introduction to differential geometry, vols. 1–6.

Physical

  1. Physics proper.

    L. Landau, E. Lifshitz, L. Pitaevskii, Course of theoretical physics, vols. 1–10.

  2. Biophysics & mathematical biology.

    W. Bialek, Biophysics: searching for principles.

Biological

  1. School-level.

    The 'Bible' of (Olympiad) biology is the famous N. Campbell, J. Reece et al., Campbell biology, archive link; the modern editions, however, have way too many autors and have been overedited, losing both flavour and pedagogical reasoning. I suggest reading an edition no later than the 7th; little information has become outdated in the 19 years since 2005. In my opinion, the book struggles with genetics, for which I recommend R. Cummings, W. Klug et al., Concepts of genetics, 12th ed., archive link (10th ed.).
